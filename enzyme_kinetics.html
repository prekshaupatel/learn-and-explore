Enzyme Kinetics

Enzymes are biological catalysts that alter the rate of reactions. The study of the effect of enzymes on the rate of these reactions is termed as enzyme kinetics.

How do enzymes work?

Enzymes speed up a reaction by lowering the activation energy (Ea) for a reaction. Since a lower activation energy reduces the energy barrier, it helps speed up the reaction.

Note: Enzymes have no effect on ΔG (Gibbs Free Energy of the Reaction), i.e., the net energy released or gained during the reaction is unchanged.

Michaelis–Menten Kinetics

The Michaelis–Menten equation can be used to predict the rate of product formation in enzyme-catalyzed reactions.

Model

The proposed model depicts the relation between the enzyme (E), substrate (S), enzyme-substrate complex (ES), and product (P):

Additionally, we know that the free enzyme concentration [E] is equal to the total enzyme concentration [Etotal minus the enzyme-substrate concentration [ES], i.e.,

[E] = [E]total - [ES] (1)

Steady-state [ES] Equilibrium

As the reaction approaches steady state, ES concentration becomes constant, i.e.,

d[ES]/dt
= 0
Thus, kf [E][S] = (kr + kcat) [ES] (2)

kf ([E]total - [ES])[S] = (kr + kcat) [ES]

kf [E]total[S] - kf [ES][S] = (kr + kcat) [ES]

kf [E]total[S] = kf [ES][S] + (kr + kcat) [ES]

Summary

Definitions

V0 is the velocity of the reaction at a particular substrate and enzyme concetration.

Vmax is the maximum velocity of the equation. This is the velocity of the reaction when the enzyme is completely utilized.

KM is the substrate concentration at with V0 is equal to half of Vmax.
